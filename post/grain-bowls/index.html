Grain bowls

Hannah ,  November 6, 2020

Hi friends!

Today, I want to introduce to you the meal made literally every week in this house, often more than once. It is so simple and yet always a winner with kids and adults alike.

Step 1: Pick your protein

The protein can be anything from chicken, ground meat, tempeh, eggs, edamame, chickpeas, lentils, tempeh, tofu or fish. We typically go for roasted tofu, but it’s also the perfect opportunity to use up leftover meat from any previous meals or BBQ nights. Roasted chickpeas with a little oil, salt and cumin is also a favourite.

Step 2: Pick your grain

Our go to is quinoa, but it’s fun to mix it up by using farro, bulgur, wheat berries, rice or any other grain you can think of! If you’d rather the base be greens, then that works too. Spinach, kale, cabbage, beet greens, arugula, basically anything except iceberg lettuce, which doesn’t hold up well under toppings. Another option would be roasted potatoes or sweet potatoes.

Step 3: Pick your veggies

Who doesn’t love roasted veggies? They are awesome for baby led weaning and most kids will try anything roasted with oil and salt. Consider broccoli, cauliflower, brussel sprouts, asparagus, beets, zucchini and so much more. If you can’t bear to turn on the oven, then go for thinly sliced raw veggies like carrots, radishes, cucumber, red cabbage, bell peppers, and whatever else you may have on hand. You can toss some green onions or fresh herbs on top, like cilantro or parsley! Sesame seeds or nuts are also really good toppers.

Step 4: Pick your sauce

Here is where I almost always use the same sauce. It’s a miso based sauce, which is a fermented soy product with a ton of flavour. Most large grocery stores have it nowadays, but for sure you can find it at most Asian grocery stores (99 Ranch, for example). Just mix up 2 tbsp tahini, 2 tbsp sesame oil, 1 tbsp honey, 2 tbsp miso, 4 tbsp rice vinegar, 1 clove minced garlic and 1 tbsp finely chopped ginger. An avocado based sauce, pesto or green goddess type sauce works just as well. When I can’t be bothered, I might just break out hoisin and sriracha sauces.

So, there you go. Four steps to the easiest meal, especially for families with picky eaters. Here is how my picky eater arranges his “bowl”.

He doesn’t want anything to touch, but he will eat every bite and uses the sauce as a dipper. If I haven’t made miso sauce or peanut sauce, then he’ll go for ketchup as a dipper. My less picky kids will usually follow his lead but are also ok with mixing it all together.

Let me know how you build your next bowl!
